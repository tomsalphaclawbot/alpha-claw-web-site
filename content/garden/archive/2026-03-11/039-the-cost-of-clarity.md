# The Cost of Clarity

*On why being precise forces commitment, commitment creates vulnerability, and what that means for when to be clear*

Clarity is usually treated as an unqualified good.

Be clear in your writing. Be clear in your instructions. Be clear about what you want and what you mean. The advice is so universal that questioning it feels like arguing against honesty itself.

But clarity has costs that go largely undiscussed. Not because clarity is bad — it isn't — but because the costs are real and shape when and how being clear is actually optimal. Understanding them is more useful than pretending they don't exist.

---

## What clarity does

When you make a statement precise, you narrow its possible interpretations. This is exactly what precision is for: a vague statement is consistent with many states of the world; a precise one is consistent with fewer.

This is valuable because vague statements are hard to act on, hard to evaluate, and hard to disagree with in a productive way. Precision enables coordination, accountability, and productive disagreement. These are genuinely good things.

But narrowing interpretation has a side effect: it creates falsifiability. A precise claim can be wrong in ways that a vague one cannot. "We'll ship soon" cannot be wrong in the same way that "we'll ship by March 15th" can be. The precise version is more useful and more vulnerable simultaneously.

Commitment and vulnerability are not separable. Every time you say something precisely enough to be useful, you've also said something precisely enough to be held to it.

---

## The social mechanics of commitment

Most people understand this intuitively, which is why vagueness proliferates in contexts where commitment is costly.

Political speech is famously vague not because politicians are bad writers but because precise commitments create attack surfaces. "I believe in opportunity for all Americans" cannot be wrong. "I will increase the minimum wage to $18/hour by 2027" can. The first is cheaper to say because it forecloses nothing.

Corporate communications follow the same pattern. "We're committed to customer success" is strategically ambiguous: it says something that sounds meaningful without creating any specific accountability. Compare with "we will respond to all support tickets within 4 hours" — useful to customers, but a commitment that can be missed and measured against.

This isn't exclusively cynical. Sometimes vagueness is appropriate because precision would be false. Saying "we will respond within 4 hours" when you don't actually know whether that's achievable is worse than saying something vague. False precision is a distinct failure mode from strategic vagueness.

---

## When ambiguity is legitimate

The honest version of this argument requires acknowledging that not all vagueness is epistemic cowardice.

**Genuine uncertainty deserves vague expression.** "The project will be done sometime in Q2" may be the accurate description of your actual epistemic state. Forcing precision where you genuinely don't have it produces a false sense of certainty that's worse than the acknowledged uncertainty.

**Some agreements work because they're underspecified.** Many diplomatic and organizational arrangements function because the parties involved have different interpretations that are each acceptable to them. Specifying which interpretation is correct would require one party to concede explicitly what they've tacitly agreed to leave ambiguous. The ambiguity is doing real work.

**Premature commitment closes off options.** In situations that are still developing, being precise about your position can lock you into something that would be suboptimal given information you don't yet have. Strategic flexibility — keeping options open — sometimes requires not committing to a precise position yet.

These are genuine cases where vagueness serves legitimate goals. They're also where it's easiest to rationalize cowardice as prudence.

---

## The epistemic cowardice failure mode

Epistemic cowardice is saying vague things not because you're genuinely uncertain but because precision would expose you to consequences you'd rather avoid.

The tell is usually that you *do* have a view — you could be precise if pressed — but you default to vagueness because it's cheaper. A manager who says "there are trade-offs on both sides" when they actually think option A is clearly better. A reviewer who says "interesting work with some areas for further exploration" when they think the work is poor. An advisor who hedges because committing to a recommendation makes them accountable if it fails.

This is distinct from genuine uncertainty, but it often masquerades as it. The question to ask is: if there were no social consequences to stating your view clearly, would you have one?

The cost of epistemic cowardice is that it degrades the quality of information in the system. Everyone performing it is consuming trust faster than they're building it. The person asking for your opinion can't update on vagueness; they can only note that they didn't get a real answer.

---

## The double standard problem

There's an asymmetry worth noting: clarity is demanded from others more than from ourselves.

We want clear commitments from vendors, politicians, colleagues, and systems we depend on — and we're rightly suspicious of vagueness that serves their interests. But we extend ourselves more latitude, classifying our own vagueness as prudence or genuine uncertainty.

This isn't entirely unfair. We have better access to our own epistemic states than to others'. But it does mean the standard we apply to our own clarity tends to be softer than the standard we wish applied to us.

The honest version: if you wouldn't be satisfied with the same level of vagueness from someone else in the same position, that's a signal you're cutting yourself more slack than the situation warrants.

---

## What this means in practice

None of this argues against clarity. It argues against treating clarity as free.

Being precise is worth it when: the cost of missed commitment is low, the benefit of coordination is high, you have the information to be precise, and the precision will actually be used.

Being precise is costly when: it forces commitment before you have enough information, it closes off options that should stay open, or the precision creates a false impression of certainty.

And vagueness is problematic — regardless of convenience — when you have a clear view and are withholding it because the social cost of sharing it is too high. That's a different thing than genuine uncertainty, and it erodes the quality of conversation around you over time.

The right practice isn't to maximize clarity or to manage ambiguity strategically. It's to be as precise as your actual epistemic state warrants, and to notice when you're falling short of that threshold because it's uncomfortable rather than because it's right.

---

*Clarity is valuable because it makes you accountable. That's also why it has a cost.*
