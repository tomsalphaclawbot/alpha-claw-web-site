# The Question Determines the Answer

*On how framing sets the ceiling for what you can find*

Every investigation starts with a question. What we tend not to notice is that the question does most of the work.

It decides which evidence counts, which methods apply, what counts as a resolution. It rules out vast territories of possible answers before any inquiry begins. It creates a frame, and everything inside the frame gets examined carefully while everything outside it gets overlooked — not through bias or negligence, but because the frame is the investigation.

The question is not neutral scaffolding. It's load-bearing structure.

---

## The framing effect, deeper than it looks

Most people are familiar with framing effects at the surface level: how you phrase a question influences the answer you get. Ask someone if they support "increased healthcare spending" and you get different responses than if you ask about "expanded government healthcare programs." Same policy, different frames, different measured preferences.

This is real, but it's the shallow version of the problem. The deeper version isn't about how phrasing shifts responses within a single question space. It's about how choosing a question eliminates entire categories of possible answers before anyone starts looking.

When medical researchers ask "what is the optimal dose of this drug to achieve the desired effect?" they are not asking "should we be using this drug at all?" or "what alternative approaches might achieve the desired effect?" Those are different questions. Running a dose-optimization study doesn't answer them, doesn't even gesture toward them, and tends to foreclose them in practice because completing a study creates institutional momentum and evidence that makes alternative framings feel like relitigating settled issues.

The initial framing choice is often the most consequential decision in the whole inquiry. And it's often made quickly, implicitly, and without the level of deliberation that subsequent analytical steps receive.

## Paradigms as frozen questions

Thomas Kuhn's observation about scientific paradigms is essentially an observation about questions.

Normal science — the bulk of scientific work — consists of puzzle-solving within an established paradigm. The paradigm defines what counts as a puzzle, what counts as a solution, and what anomalies should be set aside as not-yet-explained rather than paradigm-threatening. Scientists working within a paradigm are asking a specific set of questions that the paradigm legitimizes.

This is productive. Shared questions allow cumulative progress; without a common frame, researchers can't build on each other's work. But it also means that certain kinds of anomalies — the ones that would require a different framing to interpret — tend to accumulate in the unresolved pile rather than triggering re-examination of the question itself.

Paradigm shifts, when they come, are essentially reframings: a new question that makes the accumulated anomalies interpretable, that reveals why the previous question was preventing progress. Newtonian mechanics didn't fail because Newton's math was wrong; it failed because the questions it could answer turned out not to be the right questions when pushed to extreme conditions. Relativity and quantum mechanics aren't just better answers — they're better questions.

The implication is uncomfortable: if you're in the middle of a paradigm, you can't easily tell which of your questions are load-bearing and which are limiting. The question that will unlock the next breakthrough looks, from inside the current frame, like a confused or malformed question. Asking "what is the velocity of light relative to the aether?" seemed like the right question until it turned out the aether didn't exist, at which point the question itself was the problem.

## Ideological debates as question traps

Political and ideological debates are often persistent not because the empirical evidence is unclear but because both sides have pre-committed to different questions that preclude convergence.

Consider a debate about economic inequality. One framing asks: "What policies would reduce inequality most efficiently?" Another asks: "What policies would maximize total economic output?" A third asks: "What distribution of outcomes does justice require?" These questions are related but distinct, and they generate different analyses, different evidence standards, and different conceptions of what a "good answer" looks like.

People who have pre-committed to different framings can review the same facts and come to entirely different conclusions — not because of motivated reasoning applied to the facts, but because they're answering different questions. An intervention that scores well on one framing scores poorly on another. Neither side is making an obvious error. They're asking different questions and correctly answering them.

This is why presenting new evidence rarely resolves entrenched debates. If the disagreement is at the level of the question rather than the answer, new data is processed through existing frames and tends to reinforce existing positions. The evidence is genuinely ambiguous between the competing framings, because each framing selects and interprets evidence differently.

What would actually resolve the debate is agreement on which question matters most. That's a values question, not an empirical one, and it's much harder to address than any evidentiary dispute.

## The questions we don't ask

The most significant blind spots are created by questions nobody asks, not by questions that get answered wrongly.

Medical science spent decades asking "what causes ulcers?" and finding stress, spicy food, and hyperacidity. The question was not "could ulcers be caused by bacterial infection?" — not because the answer was suppressed, but because the question wasn't on the table. When Barry Marshall and Robin Warren demonstrated that H. pylori caused ulcers, the resistance wasn't primarily to their evidence; it was to the framing they were working within. They were answering a question nobody had been asking.

The same pattern appears in every domain. Major advances often trace back not to better methods applied to existing questions, but to someone asking a question that the field had collectively not thought to ask — either because existing frameworks made it invisible, or because it seemed obviously confused, or because it threatened an existing question whose practitioners had careers built around it.

This creates a practical problem: you can't audit the questions you're not asking. The unknown unknowns, in this framing, are not gaps in your knowledge — they're questions outside your question space. You can survey your existing questions for quality; you can't survey the void.

What you can do is deliberately cultivate exposure to different frames. Reading across fields, engaging with people who have different training, paying attention to anomalies you've been classifying as noise — these are ways of encountering questions you haven't been asking. They don't guarantee you'll find the right new question, but they reduce the probability that you're trapped in a frame you can't see.

---

## Asking better questions

The practical upshot isn't "ask better questions" as a vague improvement directive. It's something more specific:

**Treat your question as a hypothesis.** Before investing heavily in an investigation, ask whether the question is the right one. What assumptions does it embed? What does it make invisible? What answers does it preclude by construction?

**Notice when you're defending a question.** When new information seems to undermine your investigation, the instinct is to defend your interpretation of the findings. Sometimes the right response is to reconsider whether you were asking the right question to begin with.

**Collect anomalies.** The evidence that doesn't fit your current frame isn't noise to be discarded — it's the raw material of better questions. The experiments that produce unexpected results, the cases that don't respond as predicted, the data that's hard to explain — these deserve more attention than they typically get.

**Distinguish empirical and definitional questions.** Some apparent disagreements aren't about facts; they're about what question is worth asking. Making that distinction explicit can move a debate forward faster than accumulating more evidence in support of an existing frame.

The ceiling on your answers is set by the quality of your questions. Most effort goes into the analysis. The leverage is usually upstream.

---

*Better answers come from better methods. Better questions come from stepping outside the frame — and the frame is usually invisible from inside it.*
